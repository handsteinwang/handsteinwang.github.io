Pharmaceutical Trends

The quiet withdrawal of imported medicines from China’s public hospitals has become impossible to ignore. The issue has repeatedly surged on social media, not because of a single dramatic announcement, but because patients have begun noticing a more unsettling reality: medicines that were once routinely available have suddenly disappeared from hospital shelves.

Many people see December 12, 2024 as a turning point. On that day, the results of what industry observers called the most extraordinary round yet of the tenth batch of the National Centralized Volume-Based Pharmaceutical Procurement were released. The outcome was striking. Manufacturers of originator medicines either refused to participate or submitted only symbolic bids. In the end, not a single originator-medicine company won.

By January this year, patients in many places were already encountering the consequences. Imported medicines that hospitals had previously supplied on a stable basis were suddenly out of stock. What is happening is not a formal ban, nor an overnight disappearance, but a gradual retreat. Yet the practical result may be the same: patients are increasingly being pushed to switch, sometimes mid-treatment, from imported medicines to domestic substitutes.

This development is often misread. The withdrawal of foreign medicines is not mainly the result of multinational pharmaceutical companies voluntarily abandoning China, nor of geopolitical pressure from their home governments. It is, more fundamentally, the product of policy. The Chinese state has been deliberately reshaping the pharmaceutical market to favor domestic industry, and the logic behind this shift is not new. It closely resembles the path China has followed in other sectors: first attract foreign capital, technology, and products; then, once domestic firms are stronger, reduce the space available to foreign competitors.

Historically, that approach had its own logic. In the early decades after the founding of the PRC, China’s pharmaceutical manufacturing and research capacity was weak, and the overall level of healthcare and public health was low. Under those conditions, importing advanced medicines and attracting multinational pharmaceutical firms was both rational and beneficial. It improved treatment, saved lives, and gave Chinese firms an opportunity to learn from more advanced producers.

That was why China spent many years actively encouraging foreign participation in the pharmaceutical sector. Imported patented medicines and originator products were brought in, hospitals benefited from selling them, doctors often preferred them, and multinational companies found the Chinese market highly attractive. The result was a mutually reinforcing cycle: foreign medicines entered in greater numbers, patients gained access to better therapies, and the market grew.

But that was only one side of the story. China never gave up on generics. Even while welcoming foreign medicines, it steadily supported domestic generic production through both policy incentives and regulatory arrangements. Over time, local firms expanded rapidly by following the path of imitation.

The numbers make that clear. According to the 2017 Annual Statistical Report on Food and Pharmaceutical Supervision, by the end of November 2017 more than 90% of China’s 4,376 pharmaceutical manufacturers were generic-drug producers, and more than 95% of the nearly 170,000 approval numbers issued were for generic medicines. By then, the domestic industry had already become overwhelmingly centered on generics. From the standpoint of industrial policy, the role multinational firms had once played was becoming less essential.

That helps explain what followed. Since 2019, China has released three batches of catalogues encouraging generic medicines and has increasingly directed policy support toward domestic substitutes. At the same time, the market space for originator medicines has steadily narrowed. Their exclusion is not accidental. It is structural.

The obvious question is why this process has become so forceful. Why not leave more time for domestic firms to grow? Why not allow innovative medicines to compete on their merits and gradually replace foreign products through market competition? Why move so aggressively?

The answer appears to lie in the mounting pressure on medical-insurance financing. The gap in funding has grown too large, and cost control has become urgent. In many cities, the pooled funds for urban employee medical insurance are reportedly under severe strain. Transparency around the numbers has also declined, but the broader pattern is not hard to see.

The pressure can be inferred from policy changes. In August last year, the National Healthcare Security Administration and other departments issued the Notice on Effectively Carrying Out Basic Medical Security Work for Urban and Rural Residents in 2024, stating that both fiscal subsidies and individual contributions would rise again. The minimum standard reached 670 yuan in government subsidies and 400 yuan in individual contributions per person per year. This year, the financing standard rose again, reaching 1,070 yuan in total. It has increased year after year.

That alone suggests how heavy the pressure has become. And even these increases appear insufficient to close the funding gap. Under such circumstances, centralized procurement is not merely an administrative reform. It is a fiscal instrument. Its core purpose is to cut spending for the medical-insurance system, and it does so by forcing drug prices sharply downward.

The tenth batch of centralized procurement made that unmistakable. According to industry figures, the average price cut was around 70%, with some products seeing reductions of more than 90%. Enteric-coated aspirin tablets were bid at three fen per tablet. Phloroglucinol injection was quoted as low as 0.2 yuan per vial. Potassium chloride injection was quoted at 0.16 yuan per vial. Many injectable products ended up priced below one yuan.

At those levels, originator-medicine manufacturers simply cannot compete. That is not because they are unwilling to lower prices in principle, but because their cost structure is fundamentally different. Originator medicines carry the burden of long research cycles, costly clinical trials, and a high probability of failure. Developing a new medicine often takes around fifteen years and can cost several billions of U.S. dollars. The price of a successful medicine must also absorb the cost of many failed attempts.

Generic medicines operate on a different model. Once patent protection expires, they can reproduce existing compounds without bearing the original research burden. That is why they are much cheaper. But lower prices do not automatically mean identical therapeutic effect, even if the official language stresses equivalence.

This is where the debate becomes most sensitive. Officially, centrally procured domestic generics are said to have passed consistency evaluation and to be clinically reliable. In practice, however, many patients and doctors remain unconvinced.

A widely circulated case last year involved a child hospitalized with mycoplasma pneumonia. The hospital no longer had the imported azithromycin injection it had previously used and could offer only a domestic alternative. After two days, the child’s high fever had still not subsided. Only after the family managed to obtain the imported medicine did the condition improve. Stories like this have resonated because they align with a growing body of anecdotal dissatisfaction: antihypertensive drugs that seem less effective, anesthetics that do not work as expected, laxatives that fail to produce the intended result.

More importantly, such concerns are no longer limited to patients. During the 2025 Shanghai local two sessions, 20 members of the Shanghai CPPCC jointly submitted a proposal, arguing that the efficacy of some centrally procured medicines appeared unstable in clinical practice, particularly for antihypertensives, diabetes medicines, bowel-preparation laxatives, and anesthetics. Lu Changlin, a Beijing CPPCC member and chief physician at Beijing Chaoyang Hospital, made similar remarks, saying that doctors generally regarded centrally procured medicines as less effective than imported or originator products.

Once such doubts begin to spread among clinicians themselves, the issue can no longer be dismissed as consumer bias or nostalgia for foreign brands. It becomes a question of public trust in the healthcare system.

Why, then, does the gap remain so visible if consistency evaluation is supposed to guarantee comparability? One possible answer is that passing an evaluation is not the same as maintaining quality over time. Once medicines are approved and enter the market, supervision is fragmented across different local authorities. Under fragmented oversight, standards may not be enforced evenly. And where incentives are distorted, formal compliance may not guarantee real-world consistency.

That problem cannot be separated from the broader issue of corruption. Anti-corruption campaigns in the healthcare sector have been running for years, yet few people seriously believe corruption has disappeared. Pharmaceutical companies report enormous annual sales expenses, sometimes in the billions or even tens of billions of yuan. It is difficult to believe that all of this goes to ordinary expenses such as logistics, advertising, or routine operations. If anti-corruption had truly solved the problem, there would have been no need for repeated official calls to intensify the campaign.

There is also a more basic economic point. When prices are pushed down to the floor, pharmaceutical companies must preserve margins somewhere. If they cannot do so through sales prices, they will do so through costs. That means cheaper raw materials, cheaper excipients, and a production process optimized for survival rather than excellence. Within the industry, this is hardly a secret. But once the pursuit of low cost becomes absolute, the risk to quality is obvious.

Even without corruption, local protectionism can produce similar distortions. A pharmaceutical company that performs well locally may become an important taxpayer. Local governments then have an incentive to shield it from overly strict scrutiny. Under such conditions, the medicine submitted for inspection and the medicine actually produced at scale may not be identical in quality. The troubling point is not that such outcomes are guaranteed, but that the system gives the public little reason for confidence.

The long-term consequences may be even more serious than the short-term shortage of imported medicines. If this trend continues, the incentive to develop innovative medicines will weaken still further. Pharmaceutical companies are profit-driven by nature. If even established products can barely earn a return under centralized procurement, the willingness to invest in risky, expensive research will only decline.

That would be a major loss for Chinese healthcare. China already trails the most advanced medical systems in many respects. Without sustained innovation, that gap will be harder to close. Worse still, as imported medicines retreat and foreign pharmaceutical companies reduce their presence, domestic firms will face less competition, not more. And when competition weakens, the pressure to improve weakens with it.

In the end, the real issue is not whether foreign companies make less money in China, nor whether domestic firms gain market share. The real issue is what happens to patients. If the market becomes dominated by cheaper but less trusted medicines, and if the benchmark provided by originator products disappears, patients may lose both choice and a meaningful standard of comparison.

That is the deepest irony of the current policy path. A strategy designed to save money and strengthen domestic industry may ultimately weaken the quality of treatment itself. And when that happens, the greatest cost will not be paid by pharmaceutical companies, but by the people who depend on medicines most.
