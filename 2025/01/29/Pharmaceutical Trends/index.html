Pharmaceutical Trends

Recently, the topic of imported drugs withdrawing from public hospitals has attracted widespread attention and has repeatedly topped the trending search rankings. The attitude of foreign pharmaceutical companies toward the Chinese market has become increasingly negative, and the situations of business contraction, layoffs, and asset sales have become more and more frequent. December 12, 2024 can be regarded as an important point in time. On that day, the results of what was called within the industry the most unprecedented in history, the tenth batch of the National Centralized Volume-Based Drug Procurement, were announced. That is, the results of the centralized procurement were released, and all innovator drug companies either directly gave up bidding or merely quoted a symbolic price and went through the motions. In short, the final result was that not a single innovator drug company won the bid. In January this year, many people found when going to hospitals for treatment that imported drugs which had previously been supplied steadily were suddenly out of stock. It is now becoming increasingly difficult to obtain imported drugs, and in the future they may no longer be available for prescription at all, leaving patients only able to switch midway to domestic drugs. Imported drugs seem to have disappeared in this silent way.

In fact, foreign drugs’ voluntary abandonment of the Chinese market was not because these pharmaceutical companies wanted to give up the Chinese market, nor because their governments forced them to leave, but because it was accomplished under the deliberate promotion of the relevant Chinese authorities. This was part of a grand strategy by the Chinese government to promote the development of domestic industry. From the initial active attraction and encouragement of sales to the exclusion seen in recent years, this approach is in fact exactly the same as the route previously used to promote traditional manufacturing. In the early years after the founding of the PRC, China’s overall pharmaceutical production and R&D capacity were extremely backward, the overall level of medical and health care was very low, and the country’s major health indicators once ranked among the lowest in the world. If China had tried to rely solely on its own strength to research from scratch those medicines that were already mature in Western countries, it would have been both too difficult and entirely unnecessary. Therefore, China first attracted those advanced international pharmaceutical companies and innovator drugs into the country. Once they entered China, whether they built factories for manufacturing or carried out R&D, all of it was beneficial: it could both cure diseases and save lives, while also allowing Chinese pharmaceutical companies to learn the relevant technologies. For this reason, China began actively attracting foreign capital into the pharmaceutical sector, especially by introducing foreign patented drugs and innovator drugs.

In the past, due to policy-level encouragement, together with the fact that imported drugs sold at high prices and the market was good, hospitals could make enormous profits, and doctors would basically also encourage patients to use imported drugs. Imported drugs sold well in China and operated smoothly, so naturally they became increasingly fond of the Chinese market, and more good imported drugs entered the Chinese market, gradually forming a kind of virtuous cycle. However, China never relaxed its emphasis on generic drugs. Policy encouragement was one aspect, while the relevant regulatory measures were also continuously intensified in step with the development of domestic generic drugs, giving pharmaceutical companies ample room to grow. Domestic Chinese pharmaceutical companies advanced very rapidly along the path of imitation.

According to the 2017 Annual Statistical Report on Food and Drug Supervision, as of the end of November 2017, more than 90% of the country’s 4,376 pharmaceutical manufacturers were generic drug companies, and of the nearly 170,000 approved drug numbers that had been issued, more than 95\% were generic drugs. Since generic development had already progressed to about this point, and most drugs could more or less be imitated, international pharmaceutical companies were no longer all that useful, and discarding them once their purpose had been served naturally became the logical next step. Thus, from 2019 to the present, China has formulated three batches of encouraged generic drug catalogues and has begun to mainly promote domestic generic drugs. At the same time, however, innovator drugs have been squeezed out everywhere, and their room for survival has become smaller and smaller.

But why be so aggressive? Why not leave more time for our pharmaceutical companies to grow? Besides generic drugs, why not also develop more independently researched new drugs, more naturally seize market share, and gradually squeeze foreign capital out? Wouldn’t that be better? In fact, the reason is that the gap in medical insurance funds is too large and there is no time left. As the pooled funds for urban employee medical insurance in more and more cities are exhausted, the transparency of the relevant data has also become lower and lower. Even so, we can still see the seriousness of the problem from many phenomena. For example, was not the previous new medical reform intended to lock up as much money as possible in personal accounts, or to divert it for use by the pooled fund? Also, in August last year, the National Healthcare Security Administration and other departments issued the Notice on Effectively Carrying Out Basic Medical Security Work for Urban and Rural Residents in 2024, which mentioned that in 2024 governments at all levels would continue to increase subsidies for resident medical insurance enrollment contributions, with fiscal subsidies and individual contribution standards each increasing by 30 yuan and 20 yuan respectively over the previous year, reaching no less than 670 yuan and 400 yuan per person per year. This year, the minimum individual contribution standard for resident medical insurance has increased again to 400 yuan. Together with the fiscal subsidy, the financing standard has reached 1,070 yuan. It rises every year, and rising this sharply says a great deal. But even doing this is still far from enough to fill the huge hole in medical insurance. Therefore, as the medical insurance gap has expanded, centralized procurement has also become more and more frequent, forcing pharmaceutical companies to accept extremely low profits or even losses. The ultimate purpose of entering centralized procurement is to save money for medical insurance. As mentioned earlier, the magnitude of price cuts in the tenth batch of centralized procurement was the greatest in history, with an average price reduction of around 70%, and price reductions of more than 90% for multiple products. For example, the bid-winning unit price of enteric-coated aspirin tablets was three fen per tablet; the lowest quotation for phloroglucinol injection was 0.2 yuan per vial; the lowest quotation for potassium chloride injection was 0.16 yuan per vial; and the selected prices of many injectable products were all below one yuan. At such low prices, innovator drugs simply cannot accept the terms. As the pioneers of the pharmaceutical industry, innovator drugs have enormous development costs. It usually takes around 15 years to conduct research and clinical trials, and success is not guaranteed merely by spending time and money; one must also bear the cost of failure. Therefore, there is some justification for the high pricing of innovator drugs, because all of those costs must be included. Generally speaking, the investment required to develop a new drug amounts to several billions of U.S. dollars. Generic drugs, by contrast, are copycat alternatives produced by other companies after the patent protection period of innovator drugs has expired. In effect, they have no R&D costs, so their prices are certainly more affordable, although their efficacy is not necessarily reproduced one hundred percent.

Although domestic drugs are said to have the same efficacy, in fact they are simply not the same; the difference in effect is enormous. A widely circulated story last year recounted how a parent took a child to the hospital, where the child had mycoplasma pneumonia. The imported azithromycin injection previously prescribed by the hospital was no longer available, and only domestic azithromycin could be used. Left with no choice, the parent used the domestic azithromycin, but after two days of treatment the child’s high fever still would not come down. Only after great difficulty did they finally obtain the imported drug, and only then did the child’s condition improve. There are many other such examples. Blood pressure does not come down, anesthetics do not induce sleep, laxatives do not induce bowel movements—these features have already become the characteristics of domestic drugs. In short, there is only one conclusion: the efficacy is too poor. Even doctors themselves are complaining about the efficacy of domestic drugs. During the 2025 local two sessions, 20 members of the Shanghai CPPCC jointly submitted a proposal, stating that in clinical practice they had found that the efficacy of some centrally procured drugs was unstable, especially with regard to antihypertensive drugs, diabetes drugs, laxatives used for bowel preparation before endoscopic examinations, anesthetics, and so on, for which poor efficacy was often reported in clinical use. Subsequently, Beijing CPPCC member Lu Changlin, director physician of the Department of Cardiology at Beijing Chaoyang Hospital, also said that doctors generally reported that compared with imported drugs or innovator drugs, centrally procured drugs had inferior efficacy. For example, in clinical practice it was found that using the same dosage of imported novel oral anticoagulants could effectively prevent strokes, pulmonary embolism, and so forth in patients, whereas among patients using centrally procured drugs, the weight of stroke and pulmonary embolism occurrences was relatively greater. To call it “inferior” is already a euphemism. Domestic drugs are not easy to use. If the problem were only that their efficacy lagged behind, that would be one thing, but the side effects of domestic drugs are also quite obvious.

Although the authorities have continuously emphasized consistency evaluation, saying that the generic drugs used in hospitals have all passed such evaluation and that everyone may confidently use them, why is it that in clinical practice the gap in efficacy between generic drugs and imported drugs is still so large? Although they have passed consistency evaluation, what happens after the evaluation is another matter. After drugs pass the evaluation and are marketed, supervision will be dispersed among the food and drug quality supervision and administration bureaus of each province. Once power is dispersed, the quality becomes hard to guarantee. If pharmaceutical companies spend a little money to smooth things over, they may pass quite casually. Although anti-corruption efforts in the medical sector have been carried out for 20 years, corruption has become more and more serious by the day, and the methods have become ever more varied. Where do the billions or even tens of billions in annual sales expenses of those pharmaceutical companies go? Although one cannot say that all of it goes toward corruption and bribery, it is also not very credible to say that tens of billions are all spent on conventional items such as advertising, transportation, and research. If our anti-corruption efforts had truly been so thorough, then after the anti-corruption campaign was intensified in 2023, the Central Commission for Discipline Inspection would not have once again issued a statement at the end of last year saying that medical anti-corruption needed to be upgraded. Compared with the past, pharmaceutical companies now have even more motivation to spend large sums of money greasing palms.

Under centralized procurement, the profits of pharmaceutical companies have been compressed without limit. Since they can no longer see hope in the selling price, they can only work on costs and compress costs without limit. How do they do this? Not through any sophisticated optimization of the production process, but rather by comparing the prices of suppliers of raw and excipient materials and identifying those suppliers whose quality is somewhat worse but whose prices are more cost-effective. This has now become an open secret in the pharmaceutical industry. If the quality is worse, how can the efficacy be good? Even if there were no corruption, local governments, acting out of concern for protecting their own interests, would still turn a blind eye during inspections. After all, once a pharmaceutical company sells well locally, it can transform into a major local taxpayer. In that case, any problem with it would injure local fiscal revenue, and the relevant authorities, when faced with inspection matters, would carefully weigh whether it is worth it. If they make things difficult for such companies in these places, then the probability greatly increases that the drug sent for inspection will be of one quality, while the drug produced in batches will be of another quality. The key point is that no one can say clearly whether the drugs actually given to patients are effective.

If things continue according to the current trend, then no pharmaceutical company will be willing to independently develop drugs at all. Pharmaceutical companies, being driven purely by profit, were never willing to spend money on R&D in the first place. For our level of medical care, failing to innovate would be a devastating blow. To begin with, China’s medical level already lags behind advanced international medicine. If we could rely on the power of capital, our medical technology would still have the possibility of renewal and development, and it might be possible to close that gap. But under centralized procurement, pharmaceutical companies find it even harder to make money. This has completely extinguished their enthusiasm; no one is willing to do it anymore. And now that imported drugs are withdrawing, domestic pharmaceutical companies have even fewer competitors, making them still less willing to spend money on R&D. Then would not our medical level become even weaker? Would not drug efficacy become even worse? We may not even know whether efficacy is good or not, because after multinational pharmaceutical companies withdraw, our domestic drugs will no longer have any benchmark for comparison. If pharmaceutical companies think only of imitation all day long and make no effort to improve, then with imported drugs and multinational pharmaceutical companies withdrawing from the Chinese market, the biggest victims will be the Chinese people. The logic is very simple: no matter how superb doctors’ skills may be, without drugs and equipment—the most basic weapons—even the highest medical skill cannot be brought into play.

A nation that is driven only by profit and eager for quick success and instant benefit is likewise destined thereby to perish.
