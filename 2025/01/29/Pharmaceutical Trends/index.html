Pharmaceutical Trends

Recently, the issue of imported medicines withdrawing from public hospitals has attracted widespread attention and has repeatedly topped trending search rankings. Foreign pharmaceutical companies have become increasingly pessimistic about the Chinese market, and business contraction, layoffs, and asset sales have become ever more frequent. December 12, 2024 may be regarded as a critical turning point. On that day, the results of what the industry described as the most unprecedented round in history—the tenth batch of the National Centralized Volume-Based Pharmaceutical Procurement—were announced. In other words, the centralized procurement results were released, and all companies producing originator medicines either declined to participate directly or merely submitted token quotations as a formality. In short, the final outcome was that not a single originator-medicine company won the bid. In January of this year, many people discovered when seeking treatment at hospitals that imported medicines previously supplied on a stable basis had suddenly gone out of stock. Imported medicines are now becoming increasingly difficult to obtain, and in the future they may no longer be available for prescription at all, leaving patients with no option but to switch midway to domestic medicines. Imported medicines seem to have disappeared in this silent manner.

In fact, the withdrawal of foreign medicines from the Chinese market was not because these pharmaceutical companies wished to abandon China, nor because their governments compelled them to leave, but because it was brought about through the deliberate promotion of the relevant Chinese authorities. This formed part of a broader state strategy to foster domestic industry. From the initial stage of active attraction and encouragement of sales to the exclusion seen in recent years, this approach is in fact exactly the same as the path previously used to promote traditional manufacturing.

In the early years after the founding of the PRC, China’s overall pharmaceutical manufacturing and research capacity was extremely underdeveloped, the general standard of medical and public health services was very low, and the country’s major health indicators once ranked among the worst in the world. If China had attempted to rely solely on its own capabilities to develop from scratch those medicines that were already mature in Western countries, that would have been both exceedingly difficult and entirely unnecessary. Accordingly, China first brought in those advanced international pharmaceutical companies and originator medicines. Once they entered China, whether they established manufacturing facilities or carried out research and development, the result was beneficial in every respect: such participation could both treat illness and save lives, while also allowing Chinese pharmaceutical companies to learn relevant technologies. For this reason, China began actively attracting foreign capital into the pharmaceutical sector, especially by introducing patented foreign medicines and originator medicines.

In the past, because policy strongly encouraged such participation, and because imported medicines were sold at high prices and the market was favorable, hospitals could earn enormous profits, while doctors would generally also encourage patients to use imported medicines. Imported medicines sold well in China and operated smoothly within the market, so international pharmaceutical companies naturally became increasingly attached to the Chinese market. More high-quality imported medicines entered China, gradually creating a kind of virtuous cycle. However, China never relaxed its emphasis on generic medicines. Policy support was one aspect; at the same time, relevant regulatory measures were continuously strengthened in line with the development of domestic generic medicines, giving pharmaceutical companies ample room to grow. Chinese domestic pharmaceutical companies advanced very rapidly along the path of imitation.

According to the 2017 Annual Statistical Report on Food and Pharmaceutical Supervision, as of the end of November 2017, more than 90% of the country’s 4,376 pharmaceutical manufacturers were generic-medicine producers, and of the nearly 170,000 approval numbers that had been issued, more than 95% were for generic medicines. Since generic development had already progressed to roughly this stage, and most medicines could more or less be imitated, international pharmaceutical companies were no longer all that useful. Once their role had been fulfilled, discarding them naturally became the next logical step. Thus, from 2019 to the present, China has issued three batches of catalogues encouraging generic medicines and has begun to focus primarily on promoting domestic generic medicines. At the same time, however, originator medicines have been squeezed out on all sides, and their room for survival has become smaller and smaller.

But why proceed so aggressively? Why not leave more time for domestic pharmaceutical companies to grow? Beyond generic medicines, why not also develop more independently researched innovative medicines, win market share more naturally, and gradually displace foreign capital? Would that not be preferable? In fact, the reason is that the gap in medical insurance funding has become too large, and there is no time left. As the pooled funds for urban employee medical insurance in more and more cities are exhausted, the transparency of the relevant data has likewise become lower and lower. Even so, the seriousness of the problem remains visible from many phenomena.

For example, was not the previous round of medical reform intended precisely to lock as much money as possible into personal accounts, or else divert it for use by the pooled fund? In addition, in August of last year, the National Healthcare Security Administration and other departments issued the Notice on Effectively Carrying Out Basic Medical Security Work for Urban and Rural Residents in 2024, which stated that in 2024 governments at all levels would continue to increase subsidies for resident medical insurance contributions, with fiscal subsidies and individual contribution standards each rising by 30 yuan and 20 yuan respectively over the previous year, reaching no less than 670 yuan and 400 yuan per person per year. This year, the minimum individual contribution standard for resident medical insurance has risen again to 400 yuan. Together with the fiscal subsidy, the financing standard has reached 1,070 yuan. It increases every year, and such a sharp increase speaks volumes. Yet even this remains far from sufficient to fill the enormous hole in medical insurance financing. Therefore, as the funding gap in medical insurance has expanded, centralized procurement has become more and more frequent, forcing pharmaceutical companies to accept extremely low profits or even losses. The ultimate purpose of centralized procurement is to save money for the medical insurance system.

As mentioned above, the scale of price cuts in the tenth batch of centralized procurement was the greatest in history, with an average price reduction of around 70%, and reductions of more than 90% for multiple products. For example, the winning bid price for enteric-coated aspirin tablets was three fen per tablet; the lowest quotation for phloroglucinol injection was 0.2 yuan per vial; the lowest quotation for potassium chloride injection was 0.16 yuan per vial; and the selected prices of many injectable products were all below one yuan. At such prices, originator medicines simply cannot accept the terms. As the pioneers of the pharmaceutical sector, originator medicines carry enormous development costs. It typically takes around 15 years to complete research and clinical trials, and success is by no means guaranteed merely by spending time and money; the cost of failure must also be borne. Therefore, there is some justification for the high pricing of originator medicines, because all of these costs must be included. Generally speaking, the investment required to develop a new medicine amounts to several billions of U.S. dollars. Generic medicines, by contrast, are copycat substitutes produced by other companies after the patent protection period of originator medicines has expired. In effect, they bear no research and development costs, so their prices are naturally more affordable, although their efficacy is not necessarily reproduced one hundred percent.

Although domestic medicines are said to have equivalent efficacy, in reality they are simply not the same; the difference in therapeutic effect can be enormous. A widely circulated account last year described how a parent took a child to the hospital with mycoplasma pneumonia. The imported azithromycin injection previously prescribed by the hospital was no longer available, and only domestic azithromycin could be used. With no alternative, the parent used the domestic azithromycin, yet after two days of treatment the child’s high fever still would not subside. Only after great difficulty did they finally obtain the imported medicine, and only then did the child’s condition improve. There are many other such examples. Blood pressure does not come down, anesthetics do not induce sleep, laxatives do not produce bowel movements—these have already become characteristic complaints about domestic medicines. In short, there is only one conclusion: their efficacy is too poor.

Even doctors themselves have been complaining about the efficacy of domestic medicines. During the 2025 local two sessions, 20 members of the Shanghai CPPCC jointly submitted a proposal, stating that in clinical practice they had found the efficacy of some centrally procured medicines to be unstable, especially in the case of antihypertensive medicines, diabetes medicines, laxatives used for bowel preparation before endoscopic examinations, anesthetics, and so forth, for which inadequate efficacy was frequently reported in clinical use. Subsequently, Beijing CPPCC member Lu Changlin, chief physician in the Department of Cardiology at Beijing Chaoyang Hospital, also stated that doctors generally reported centrally procured medicines to be less effective than imported medicines or originator medicines. For example, in clinical practice it was found that the same dosage of imported novel oral anticoagulants could effectively prevent stroke, pulmonary embolism, and related complications in patients, whereas among patients using centrally procured medicines, the relative weight of stroke and pulmonary embolism events was greater. To describe them as merely “inferior” is already euphemistic. Domestic medicines are not easy to use. And if the problem were only that their efficacy lagged behind, that would be one thing; the side effects of domestic medicines are also quite obvious.

Although the authorities have continuously emphasized consistency evaluation, asserting that the generic medicines used in hospitals have all passed such evaluation and that everyone may use them with confidence, why is it that in clinical practice the gap in efficacy between generic medicines and imported medicines remains so large? Although they may have passed consistency evaluation, what happens after the evaluation is another matter entirely. Once medicines pass evaluation and enter the market, supervision is dispersed among the food and pharmaceutical quality supervision and administration bureaus of the various provinces. Once power is dispersed, quality becomes difficult to guarantee. If pharmaceutical companies spend a little money to smooth things over, they may pass with great ease. Although anti-corruption campaigns in the medical sector have continued for 20 years, corruption has only grown more serious over time, and its methods have become ever more varied.

Where do the billions or even tens of billions in annual sales expenses reported by those pharmaceutical companies actually go? Although one cannot say that all of it is used for corruption and bribery, it is equally difficult to believe that tens of billions are all spent on ordinary items such as advertising, transportation, and research. If anti-corruption efforts had truly been so thorough, then after the medical anti-corruption campaign was intensified in 2023, the Central Commission for Discipline Inspection would not have once again issued a statement at the end of last year declaring that medical anti-corruption needed to be upgraded. Compared with the past, pharmaceutical companies now have even stronger incentives to spend large sums of money greasing palms.

Under centralized procurement, the profits of pharmaceutical companies have been compressed without limit. Since they can no longer find hope in the selling price, they can only work on costs and compress costs without limit. How is this done? Not through any sophisticated optimization of the production process, but by comparing suppliers of raw materials and excipients and identifying those whose quality is somewhat worse but whose prices are more cost-effective. This has already become an open secret within the pharmaceutical sector. If the quality is worse, how can the efficacy be good?

Even if corruption did not exist, local governments, acting out of concern for protecting their own interests, would still turn a blind eye during inspections. After all, once a pharmaceutical company performs well locally, it can become a major local taxpayer. In that case, any problem affecting it would damage local fiscal revenue, and the relevant authorities, when confronted with inspection matters, would carefully weigh whether such action is really worth the cost. If they create obstacles for such companies in these places, then the probability increases greatly that the medicine submitted for inspection will be of one quality, while the medicine actually produced in batches will be of another. The key point is that no one can say with certainty whether the medicines actually given to patients are truly effective.

If things continue along the current trajectory, then no pharmaceutical company will be willing to independently develop medicines at all. Pharmaceutical companies, being driven purely by profit, were never especially willing to spend money on research and development in the first place. For our level of medical care, the loss of innovation would be a devastating blow. To begin with, China’s medical standards already lag behind those of advanced international systems. If we could rely on the power of capital, our medical technology would still have the possibility of renewal and development, and it might still be possible to narrow that gap. But under centralized procurement, pharmaceutical companies find it even harder to make money. This has completely extinguished their enthusiasm; no one is willing to do it anymore.

Now that imported medicines are also withdrawing, domestic pharmaceutical companies face even fewer competitors, making them still less willing to invest in research and development. Would this not further weaken our medical standards? Would it not further worsen therapeutic efficacy? We may not even know whether efficacy is good or bad, because after multinational pharmaceutical companies withdraw, domestic medicines will no longer have any benchmark for comparison. If pharmaceutical companies spend all day thinking only about imitation and make no effort to improve, then with imported medicines and multinational pharmaceutical companies withdrawing from the Chinese market, the greatest victims will be the Chinese people. The logic is very simple: no matter how superb doctors’ skills may be, without medicines and equipment—the most basic weapons—even the highest level of medical skill cannot truly be brought into play.
